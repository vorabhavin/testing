Scaling a global COVID-19 study site in seven days, part 2: How We Did It

Scaling a global COVID-19 study site in seven days, part 2: How We Did It

by Platform DevOps

Published: Feb 10, 2021

 What do you do when you’re handed a complex problem that could affect millions of people during a pandemic, and time is running out? You pull together to solve it. Here’s how.

The Customer and Commercial Creation Center (C&CCC) team at Pfizer were tasked with making the COVID vaccine study site production ready. www.covidvaccinestudy.com informs and recruits potential participants for the most important and wide-reaching clinical trial of our time.

In Part 1, we looked at the challenges we faced - including the fact we are living in unusual times, and in response, our workflows and approaches are having to bend and flex and scale in ways we aren’t used to. On the plus side, we found that recruiting participants was not a problem. However, time was not on our side: the site had to be re-worked and launched in under a week, with many different types of problems needing to be addressed. Here’s how we did it.

Rearchitecting the site

A lot of thought goes into C&CCC projects ahead of time: not just what we’re going to use to build them, but all the interactions, where the data flows are going to happen, the sensitivity of the data, the volume of users, where the bottlenecks might be within the architecture, where we need to scale out, or at least have plans to scale out. Having the right technical approach and architecture is essential and it’s nearly impossible to retrofit this. Unfortunately, that’s what we had to do.

Two of the many examples of this:

  • Batching requests sent to the back-end: the original site was set up so that an individual would submit an expression of interest, then, in real time, the site would send that off to the back-end system which could handle a maximum of 20 concurrent requests. As we mentioned in Part 1, this would be fine in a typical clinical trial site.

However in this case, on the run up to launch day, we were told that we could possibly expect around 2 million expressions of interest. With figures this big, it’s highly likely that more than 20 would have been submitted at the same time. This is a huge problem. It’s a bit like having only 20 cashiers on duty at the universe’s biggest supermarket while thousands of people trying to pay for their groceries and get out at the same time.

In order to tackle this challenge, we implemented a batching system to provide a calm, programmatic, predictable process. Every minute, the system would send any queued expressions of interest to the back end as a single batch, so it was no longer in danger of getting swamped, or bottlenecking because it couldn’t handle the increased number of submissions.

  • Building the holding page: we also added a static holding page, which was important to the user experience. This meant that if the site was down for any reason, the individual wasn’t left facing an empty, uninformative page. These kinds of details make a big impact.

Testing

Testing is a topic close to many developers’ hearts. It goes hand-in-hand with building. If you’re writing and implementing tests at Quality Assurance (QA) stage, that’s way too late. Proper testing ensures you’re building correctly and confidently from day one, and stops you making assumptions that your code “just works”.

Here are some examples of what C&CCC included:

  • Performance testing: we were lucky to have the support and assistance of a global expert in Drupal performance. He worked with us to modify some of Drupal’s core systems and the webform module, which handles the form that the study participants fill in. This allowed us to meet the substantially higher performance objectives - for instance, following these modifications, the site could handle about 500 form submissions a second.
  • UI testing: we were able to put in UI testing that would click through the browser as if it was a human every 15 minutes and fill in the form. We could then quickly see if there was a failure using our monitoring tools, and therefore be in a position to rectify it immediately rather than waiting for an end user to report it.
  • Best practice in software testing: we combined our efforts and used every means of testing at our disposal; not just the automated testing outlined above, but peer review (checking each other’s code) and pro-active testing, rather than waiting for things to fail. These combine to create a culture of safety, and support engineering teams to be stronger when working together and supporting each other’s work, as well as the quality of the entire team’s output. This is standard practice in C&CCC.

Improving security

Though some of this came from rearchitecting the site, other parts needed specific attention. It was especially important that no Pfizer employee had any access to any PII (Personally Identifiable Information) or data about study participants.

  • Putting Cloudflare in front of the global CDN: a CDN (Content Delivery Network) is a collection of globally distributed servers which aims to optimize performance of content delivery, even though the consumers may be spread over a large geographical area. We put Cloudflare in front of our Fastly CDN to provide increased security - immediately, we gained DDoS protection (a Distributed Denial of Service attack is one of the most common forms of cyber-attack), a web application firewall, and more.
  • In addition, our infrastructure team tuned the security so that a lot of the “garbage” or noise traffic was dropped before it even reached the CDN, let alone getting anywhere near the back end.
  • A dramatic reCAPTCHA saga: reCAPTCHA is a Google-developed system which aims to prevent malicious, automated attacks by bots. You’ll be familiar with it - it asks you to enter a jazzy-looking set of letters or numbers, or - if you’re seen as a risk, for a variety of mundane reasons - click on all the tiles containing, for instance, traffic lights.
  • The original site used the open-source version of reCAPTCHA. Unfortunately, this wasn’t going to scale: we called Google and they kindly informed us that, given the amount of traffic we were expecting, the reCAPTCHA simply wasn’t going to work. At all. Which was not good. We needed to move to the Enterprise version - which, of course, none of the existing modules supported, so we refactored to make it work, pulling it together using a watertight combination of the Enterprise reCAPTCHA, Cloudflare, and an additional module called Honeypot.

The outcomes: data never lies

The few known performance objectives we had to hit were big. However, we’re really, really pleased with how this went - the numbers are staggering: Load test results:

  • Browse before - 50 page loads a second at 19s a page
  • Browse after - 5,000 page loads a second at 0.3s a page

Not only is that 100x the traffic, it’s also 55x faster.

  • Submit before - agency wouldn’t allow us to load test on submissions
  • Submit after - 550 submissions per second at 1/5th of a second each

That’s almost 2M an hour.

Thanks to the ingenuity and dedication of team and individual efforts, an incredible feat was pulled off, and we are both proud and humbled. But what did we learn from this experience?
