Scaling a global COVID-19 study site in seven days, part 1: The Challenges We Faced

Scaling a global COVID-19 study site in seven days, part 1: The Challenges We Faced

by Platform DevOps

Published: Feb 9, 2021

How a rapidly-assembled super-team across Customer and Commercial Creation Center (C&CCC) platforms collaborated to beat the odds and build Pfizer’s most high-traffic, high-profile site to date.

The COVID-19 virus has affected billions of people on the planet and changed the course of our shared future. Pfizer is at the forefront of helping the world tackle this crisis by successfully developing a vaccine at speed and at scale.

In order to launch a vaccine, Pfizer must adhere to strict regulations, including the clinical trial process. Part of this process was the development of a site that informs and recruits potential participants for the most important and wide-reaching clinical trial of our time: the race to save lives by finding a safe, effective, high-quality vaccine for the COVID-19 virus.

Over three articles, we explore the story behind the scenes; the urgency, what was implemented, and what was learned.

A COVID-19 clinical trial site isn’t like other clinical trial sites

Firstly, it must be pointed out that this project was, quite simply, unprecedented.

For those of you not familiar with clinical trials, they are both incredibly common and almost always low on recruited participants.

COVID-19 has changed all this: people are actively keen to sign up to COVID-19 vaccine trials.

While this is a scientific sociological boon, it means traditional web-based approaches to recruiting participants suddenly face new challenges:

  • Vastly increased traffic, which leads to
  • More pressure on the site to be consistently highly performant, while still
  • Maintaining a high level of security
    ​​​​​​​

Teams that build these clinical study sites usually benefit from their own economy of scale; there is a proven and problem-free template and stack for these kinds of projects. This means they can build sites and get them out to the public quickly and efficiently because the requirements have historically been so similar. This leads into our next consideration:

The existing architecture was not ideal given this project’s requirements

The COVID-19 vaccine study site was originally built by a vendor team experienced in building sites for clinical trials but underwent a dramatic and fast-paced transformation by C&CCC in the week leading up to launch.

If you check out the website for yourself, (https://www.covidvaccinestudy.com) you can see it’s simple and easy to navigate.

This kind of design is called a Single Page Application (SPA). They’re incredibly lightweight and don’t ever need to reload - they dynamically change the information displayed based on the user’s interaction.

The COVID-19 vaccine study site was originally built using Drupal 7 and hosted on Pantheon. In some senses, these are great choices:

  • Drupal is an open-source content management framework written in PHP. It’s got a strong reputation for having an engaged community and, like all the best open-source software, benefits from dedicated people constantly updating and improving it.
  • Similarly, of all the popular Drupal hosting platforms, Pantheon is the one of the most technical, and is headed up by a global expert in Drupal performance.

We know Drupal is a powerful CMS (it’s the foundation of our Edison Custom platform for building complex sites, after all). But because there’s no real CMS requirement for this study site - no digital content that needs to be updated, published, stored, reviewed collaboratively - Drupal is a little over-powered for this type of project.

Additionally, all C&CCC projects are usually hosted on Acquia, not Pantheon - so while we are fortunate to have engineers on our team who are familiar and experienced with Pantheon, it added a layer of complexity to address.

The original site wasn’t built to meet the crucial additional needs

It doesn’t need stating how important this site is - and how much scrutiny it would be under. This unusually high visibility, high profile site, when combined with the unbelievably high expected traffic, needed to be consistently flawless.

This isn’t just a matter of pride for C&CCC. The success of any project, let alone one as important as this, has far-reaching consequences for all of Pfizer. Even small projects can have big impacts, both for end users (patients, HCPs, the public using our products and services) and for the company.

A data breach because one project wasn’t tested comprehensively enough would impact not just the trial’s enrolment, but also Pfizer as a company, and negatively impact the public’s impression of pharma as a global industry.

Time was not on our side, and we had a lot of work to do

From the time it was identified that the site needed re-working to final launch was alarming: under a week.

This was compounded by the fact that not all the problems were immediately visible. At first glance, Edison immediately flagged that it didn’t meet any of our C&CCC standards… and the more they unpacked it, the more problems became apparent.

C&CCC are experts in delivering digital experiences and solutions which optimize for a multitude of factors meeting tight deadlines. We don’t believe that being pressed for time should compromise pushing for quality (security, experience, safety, scalability, performance). Especially in the pharma industry, those sacrifices aren’t acceptable. We know that different individuals in teams have competing priorities and objectives, but they must align on delivering the product when it’s ready to be delivered, at the highest quality, safe and thoroughly tested. We don’t compromise on this.

But to be totally honest: on paper, this looked impossible. At a high level, there were two big issues we needed to address, made up of myriad smaller parts:

  • Performance/scalability
  • Security
    ​​​​​​​

The word “unprecedented” is synonymous with 2020, and this was no exception. However, simplifying a huge challenge down to two main problems was a good start.

In the week that followed, we systematically broke down and solved – with a serious amount of teamwork and effort – the smaller constituent problems.
