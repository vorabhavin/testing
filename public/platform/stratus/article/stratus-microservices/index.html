Stratus Microservices: an interview with Andy Cavanagh, Microservices Team Tech Lead

Stratus Microservices: an interview with Andy Cavanagh, Microservices Team Tech Lead

by Platform DevOps

Published: Jan 29, 2021

Making novel functionality with reusable pieces: an introduction to the Stratus Microservices team and how they improve the workflow for building interactive digital solutions.

Andy, what is your role at Pfizer?

I’m the Tech Lead of the Stratus Microservices Team within Pfizer Digital.

What kinds of challenges does your team solve for the business?

The Stratus Microservices project is intrinsically linked to Edison Lite and Canvas Web Builder, and in order to answer this question it’s best to explain while including those relationships.

Historically, Pfizer Digital turned a corner when it improved its infrastructure by moving all website solutions to be built on Drupal – a fully-fledged content management system (CMS) and a very reliable industry choice. Edison has a product offering called Edison Custom and it’s exactly for these situations – custom websites which need more complex content management and other options. However, Edison Custom can be an expensive and over-engineered solution for simple static sites with just a few pages of content that won’t get updated often. A lot of Pfizer sites have these fairly straightforward specifications. To that end, they have another offering called Edison Lite which suits these needs perfectly.

As Canvas Web Builder is now integrated with Edison Lite, you can build and maintain these simple sites using a great drag and drop interface. It removes reliance on a developer team, presents fewer security concerns than a Custom site, and is cheaper and quicker to build, launch and maintain.

But what about the sites that are somewhere in between needing to be Edison Custom (Drupal) sites and Edison Lite? For instance, sites that need some kind of “interactive” functionality, like a Search ability or a specific type of Form where users enter details (a Contact form, or one to generate a Copay card). You can build this in JavaScript, but you very quickly get into non-standardized approaches which differ between vendors. If we can provide a standardized, well-maintained way to do this, we can move a bunch more sites off Edison Custom, optimizing how we build digital assets in the future.

With that understanding, the short answer is: we save the business money, we improve developer experience by providing standardized, specialized solution modules, and we improve Pfizer Digital’s digital infrastructure.

What does success look like to your team?

Improving site build time, quality, and user experience. It’s early days but we have promising metrics; 30 sites have already been migrated to using microservices and more in the pipeline.

The concept of microservices, and the implementation of them at Pfizer, is very much in line with Mike Lamb’s vision for a standardized, well-supported digital ecosystem. Businesses (and technology and time) don’t stand still – if we want to stay on top of things and continuously build excellent solutions, we need to focus on automation, scalability, and end user experience.

What sort of problems does your team face, in turn?

Holding onto the spirit of innovation that Mike pushes for in Platform DevOps has been important in our learning curve.

One of the biggest challenges we have is that we’re building in somewhat of a silo. The feedback loop is big and complex, as the chain of involvement spreads over multiple different teams. The people who give us the requirements are not the end users; between us and the end users there are many other teams – NextGen, Canvas, developers and POs and content teams in various places across the business. Then there’s the differences in project flow between new sites that are being built, and existing sites that are being migrated.

What’s something you’re especially proud of the team for?

Something I think our team solved in a really excellent way is the fundamental problem that everyone was sharing: the architecture and ecosystem of microservices. I’m really happy with how small and functional and extensible each piece is. We’ve had folks use the various microservices to build functionality that we had never intended because they’re able to string various pieces together and make novel functionality. That’s excellent.

Another thing I think the team has made headway with is how Digital interfaces with Support. We still have a way to go on this, but we came up with what we think is a good model on one of our last bespoke projects (COVID vaccine adverse effects reporting or COVAES), and we’re working on templating that and backporting it to other services.

Lastly, we’ve been writing a lot of documentation in tandem with building the microservices. Again, this is invaluable for Support, but important for all our users, and all too often gets deprioritized.

What does the future hold for the Microservices team?

We’ve currently built the UI for at least 80% of the most straightforward use cases, and they’ve been implemented in Edison Dashboard. It wouldn’t be feasible to build UI to cover every eventuality, every possible thing you could do with the Form service – but we’re thinking about how to optimize the UI given that understanding. That said, as a team we’re not huge fans of determining how people should use the system. It’s flexible and parts of it are still under development, and it’s largely up to the user to decide how they want to use it. Certain implementations of UI would restrict that.

The Microservices Team is looking forward to optimizing how Microservices are built on Stratus through automation, templating, and technical guardrails. This will make all the code for a new project fast, simple, secure, and most importantly consistent across all vendors who have been tasked with creating microservices.

The team would also like to take up building an authentication service for Edison Lite sites, like what NextGen is doing with GRV but with Cognito as the Auth Backend and microservices as the proxy/middleware.

There’s a bit of a ways to go before it’s totally self-service through Canvas, but we’re well on the way.
