Salesforce Enablement and Stratus within Pfizer 

Salesforce Enablement and Stratus within Pfizer

by Clark Wilson, Core Commercial and Medical Affairs Engineering Lead

Published: Oct 1, 2021

Clark Wilson, Core Commercial and Medical Affairs Engineering Lead within CGW Creation Center, explains the power of collaborating with Stratus

Salesforce enablement & Stratus work together

“If we’re using Stratus, we can focus more on the things that matter for our users.”

Clark, explain your background and your role at Pfizer:

My background has always been in health sciences, consulting and the insurance industry in the US. I drifted into the pharma industry and just fell in love with how interesting it is. It’s where a lot of the important transformational projects are really happening from a technology standpoint.

Over the last 12 years at Pfizer, I’ve worked mostly within the commercial, internal-facing systems with the field-based medical affairs teams and commercial operations. This means managing their sales data and metrics, customer relationship management systems (CRMs) systems, and what we call Sales Force Automation systems (SFAs) or Sales Force Enablement (SFE).

How does the SFE team work?

We are a Core Commercial and Medical Affairs (CCMA) Creation Center. We are a team made up of Functional Area Leads, Project Managers, Solution Engineers, Business Analysts, Developers, and Testers.

Because my team is so familiar with salesforce.com as a platform, we function as a Centre of Excellence for the enterprise around it. We’re also able to build a lot of internal-facing niche tools to fill gaps. We’ve worked with an incredibly diverse range of teams, from HR to R&D to global product development, to build out and deliver valuable tools on the Salesforce platform.

Do you find that’s an exciting place to be?

Absolutely. We can bring process and technological advancement to internal and external customers, and that’s exciting. It’s valuable to me and my team to know that we’re helping people - patients getting onboarded to a new oncology drug, or HCPs managing stock by taking a photo of a pharmacy shelf and the tech being able to calculate whether it matches Pfizer’s contractual agreements with that firm.

It’s really interesting how it’s moved from electronic signatures to bots to voice-enabled tech to machine learning and artificial intelligence. Technology in this space has really clear, tangible, understandable use cases.

How did you come to work with Stratus?

We started to come across use cases that didn’t quite fit on the salesforce.com platform. We needed to build a custom solution but we wanted to maintain our standards. Having clear, consistent technology, patterns, standards, and processes is important to me and my team.

In comparison to, for example, a product like Heroku, Stratus offers us this essential consistency around internal-facing web apps by default. It’s made by Pfizer for Pfizer, and it really is the best out there for our use cases.

What challenges does Stratus solve for Sales Force Enablement?

Firstly, the aforementioned consistency: it leads to shorter implementation time, more accurate estimations, and makes it easier for support to grasp.

Stratus also provides transparency - there’s no black box piece of it because we grew it ourselves.

It’s also very flexible - in nearly all cases, it provides what we need straight out of the box.

Can you give an example of a project Stratus helped Sales Force Enablement build?

We built a completely custom web app called OpenSight using Stratus. It’s a self-service, lightweight, customer categorization or segmentation tool, that’s used by commercial operations around the world. It handles a significant amount of data and helps teams engage with their customers better using statistical analysis.

I was really impressed at both the quality and the functionality of Stratus in this project. OpenSight is still live today after four years and a few iterations - it’s worked out really well.

How does Stratus benefit your users?

For our internal users, using Stratus offers multiple benefits: predictable components across projects, reduced complexity, faster to build, cheaper to run, and more efficient.

Those benefits pass all the way to the end user, too, because we’re not worrying about having to start from scratch all the time - we can spend more time and energy on the little pieces that improve the user experience, on the pieces that matter to them.

How can other teams benefit from your experience of working with Stratus?

I’d encourage teams to give it a shot for all the same reasons we started working with Stratus in the first place. It’s really important to push for those consistent standards, and help build sustainability and maintainability for the future.

Stratus provides an accessible, Pfizer way of building web apps where all the heavy lifting is already done. It’s cost effective, it’s consistent, it’s maintainable. There are standards around it and their support teams understand how to support it.

Transitioning to using Stratus has involved far less anxiety than building a completely custom application because so many of the smaller decisions were already taken care of. My learning was that, if we’re using Stratus, we can focus more on the things that matter for our users.

How do you think collaborations such as Sales Force Enablement and Stratus could help Pfizer evolve?

From a maintainability standpoint, or from an enhancement standpoint, it’s much easier to onboard someone and ask them to pick up a Stratus project as - regardless of what the project is - they’ll be familiar with Stratus as a baseline. That’s of huge benefit to the sustainability of Pfizer assets of all size.

It’s also much more likely that, using Stratus, we’ll see the lifespan of applications and projects with the SFE team stretch beyond their usual three to five years and have a much longer value stride. This means we can balance learning and delivery for longer, which is important for Pfizer to keep up with developments in engineering and architecture.

Using Stratus as a vehicle for introducing these new ideas, staying on top of new services and being open to progressing the way we do things is the game-changer piece for me, looking forward.
