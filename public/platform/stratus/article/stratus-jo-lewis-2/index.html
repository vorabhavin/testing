In conversation with: Jo Lewis, Scrum Master (Part Two) 

In conversation with: Jo Lewis, Scrum Master (Part Two)

by Platform DevOps

Published: Sep 24, 2021

In part two of our conversation with Jo Lewis - Scrum Master for Stratus - we hear more about scaling the Agile approach across Pfizer and improving delivery.

"Agile is working really well for Stratus, and this is is really relevant to the wider picture of Pfizer.”

Jo, as a member of Scrum Master Guild, can you explain the benefits of this not just for you professionally, but also Pfizer?

The Scrum Master Guild is an amazing place. Anybody in any role can be part of the Guild - we have about 70 people in total. We come together with different ideas, experiences and best practices and we share them, across teams, which means we learn from each other.

The Guild also hosts knowledge-sharing sessions on particular areas of interest, which are conducted by Pfizer subject matter experts.

You’re SAFe-certified. How would you explain SAFe to the uninitiated?

SAFe is Scaled Agile Framework. As you can infer, it takes Agile and scales it across the entire organization, allowing you to implement an Agile approach consistently across large, multi-team programs and portfolios. It will be a beautiful thing once all of Pfizer becomes SAFe because the entire portfolio will be managed in the same way, improving consistency in expected delivery.

How exactly will it help?

All teams, even if they only deliver on a specific part of the Pfizer portfolio, typically need to work with different Pfizer teams. This means there will be interdependencies between teams in order to be able to deliver.

SAFe has a quarterly planning process where teams meet, take a look at the deliverables, and see what the customer was promised. This bubbles up the dependencies: you can highlight the critical path to delivery, plus all of the issues, and everybody’s on the same page.

How do you think a SAFe approach could benefit Pfizer?

SAFe promotes working together and understanding what’s expected. When you start using an Agile approach, and especially in this scaled fashion, everyone has access to the information: you know what other teams are delivering, and you know when you’re a dependency.

When Pfizer begins to adopt Agile across the organization it will not only allow teams to deliver quickly and to be self-organizing, but also to be more customer-focused. You have the ultimate transparency. That’s brilliant.

How are you implementing SAFe in Stratus?

We’re participating in the HCP (Health Care Professionals) Agile release process which uses SAFe. By being part of this wider process, we can plan better, giving more accurate delivery timeframes which helps us build our roadmap.

This is important in terms of understanding the resources needed by the team to complete the work, and collaboration with HCP/NextGen Healthcare is ongoing during the quarter to ensure we are delivering as expected. It is very agile and iterative.​​​​​​​

There’s been some pushback against SAFe approach from Agile purists. Where do you stand on that?

Some people think SAFe isn’t agile enough. They question: how self-organizing can you be when you’re being managed? But when you have multiple products or multiple platforms - not just a start up with a single product or platform - you have to manage, and manage that portfolio, effectively.

The industry is not agile at times, so you have to bend your approach and do it the best way you can. For purists, it will never work.

What lessons have you learned from working in an agile way in Stratus?

I’ve learned how important it is to stay connected interpersonally with a remote team.

In all teams, delivery is dependent on them being satisfied or unsatisfied with their work. This means being able to work with each other, finding a way to work around challenging timezones, and making sure that the energy is positive.

Given the challenges, what culture needs to exist in order for SAFe and Agile to work?

A trusting environment: People have to trust that you have their best interests at heart, and that it’s okay to fail or change direction.

In your experience, have you found Agile creates stronger outcomes, or makes teams more productive?

It really does, because people have to work closely together to deliver all parts of it. There’s a high level of personal and collective responsibility. Teams collaborate to create a shared goal at the start of each sprint, and check in daily with each other, and for remote teams, that’s really important.

Agile is working very well for Stratus, and this is so relevant to the wider picture of Pfizer.

What advice do you have to those who are new to Agile, but want to see if it works for them?

Give it a try. Pfizer is a highly compliant, driven company. We can still adhere to all these rules, but Agile is a different approach to how we deliver, and it’s going to make the company better.

Don’t be afraid to work with other agile teams and share best practice, and with the AAO (Agile Acceleration Office). We learn, and improve, by working together.
