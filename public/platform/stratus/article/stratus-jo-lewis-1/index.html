In conversation with: Jo Lewis, Scrum Master

In conversation with: Jo Lewis, Scrum Master

by Platform DevOps

Published: Sep 17, 2021

Joann Lewis - Scrum Master for Stratus, a member of the Scrum Master Guild, and pioneer of the Agile approach within Pfizer C&CCC - tells us how Agile unlocks delivery and creates better teams.

"

In conversation with Jo Lewis

“It’s really important for Pfizer to stay ahead, and I think Agile is the best way we can do it”.

Jo, how did you become a Scrum Master for Stratus?

I’ve been in this field for over 20 years. I started out as a QA analyst, and I have a degree in computer science. Even though I never became a developer, I understand the methodology and I’ve worked closely with developers over the years.

Over time, I moved into infrastructure support, IT Service Management, software management, and then Scrum Master. Looking back, it was a transition involving working and understanding infrastructure and the software delivery lifecycle.

What is your role at Pfizer?

I oversee the development process for Stratus, and coach the team on the Agile process. I help organize the team and their work by removing roadblocks and ensuring they clearly understand the goal they’re trying to accomplish during each sprint or iteration.

For those who don’t work with Agile every day, how would you explain it?

Agile is a methodology we use at Pfizer - and in industry - to help us deliver projects faster and in a more responsive way to customer needs.

The Agile methodology is very simple: a team comes together and works on something in the same area, rather than being scattered. They then deliver something useful in a short period of time, and work with a stakeholder to confirm it’s in line with expectations.

If it isn’t, the stakeholder can flag issues so the team can amend the direction of development. It’s very nimble in its approach. Agile also promotes collaboration on the team; it’s a learning and teaching atmosphere, helping people deliver.

Are there different ways to ‘do’ agile methodology?

Yes. My team - a microservices team - are doing ScrumBan, a combination of Scrum and Kanban, which are slightly different enactments of ‘Agile’ based on the same principles.

All Agile teams work to deliver in a short period of time and iterate, making changes as they go. Kanban makes changes on a daily basis, whereas the Scrum teams use fortnightly. ScrumBan, being a hybrid, tends to be weekly.

What else do you need for Agile to work?

You can’t have an agile team without a Product Owner: they own the relationship between the technical team and the customer, and what we call the backlog - the body of work that gives the agile team their purpose. The Product Owner is the customer’s voice in an agile world.

Why is agile important to Pfizer?

Pfizer has a diverse range of customers. Using Agile, we can deliver to them much faster. In addition, we know we’re actually delivering what they want because we’re soliciting their input along the way. It allows us to work smarter, and to learn, as we’re working ensuring we recover from mistakes more quickly.

How does agile help Stratus customers?

In Stratus, our internal customers are delivering for other internal customers or for external customers. Sometimes, the requirements evolve, and when stakeholders demand change, we’re right there with our internal customers working that feedback in. It’s really important for Pfizer to stay ahead, and I think the use of Agile in Stratus is a great example of how we can do that well.

What kind of support have you had from your team to enable the agile approach to be adopted?

It’s really important for managers to be enablers: to accept the process and not want to water it down. Jamie Hook (Stratus Platform Lead) is 100% on board with Agile, which really helps.

When I arrived at Pfizer, the company didn’t do Agile. I had to create and work with the team directly to adopt the process. But Jamie was all for it, and he made sure we had what we needed.

Given your experience, do you think there are common challenges across Pfizer?

Yes. I think when you have huge, complex projects involving multiple teams, it’s really positive to have a programme manager or architect that stays at the very top.

The complexity stems from communication. On our project, it took the different teams some time to be able to work effectively together and understand the scope of each other’s work. We really could have used a Pfizer architect who understood not just the platforms, but also the needs right down the chain.

Another challenge was interdependencies between teams, blocking delivery. However, I’m also certified to use SAFe - the Scaled Agile Framework - which takes the beauty of the Agile approach and scales it across the entire organization. This would help with predictability and lessen these interdependencies.
