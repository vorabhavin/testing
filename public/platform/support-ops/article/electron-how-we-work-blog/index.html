Electron: How we work and design our systems

Electron: How we work and design our systems

by Anastasiia Holoviznina, Electron Developer Advocate

Published: Mar 22, 2021

A closer look at the platform that’s automating Pfizer’s digital solutions

​​​​​​​​​​​​​

How we work and design our systems

In the previous post Dick provided lots of information about the importance of the Electron team’s work. This time, we’re going to slightly open the curtain of our team structure, explain how we work and how we design our systems.

How we work

When we started, we were one big Electron team but as Pfizer’s needs have evolved it was decided to split the team in 3 parts: Electron, Service Reliability Engineering (SRE) and Biblio.

This means that each team has their own responsibility: Electron improves automation and supports current processes, SRE lends support and works on high level dependencies (libraries, base services, etc.) and Biblio works on top of the Electron platform for gathering data about Pfizer’s assets (web sites, mobile apps, social accounts etc.) and provides access to the data in a single API.

Each of the teams has its own team lead. As a result, we have 3 well-coordinated teams that have good communication and understand their place in the big chain.

To keep good communication in the team we have couple of “rules”. The main rule is to be open – for discussions, investigating issues, getting new knowledge etc. so that we can discover issues before people complain about them. The big advantage here is that it helps us to provide a quality product and support so if you have any issues, you can always contact us where we are open to help you.

The processes in teams are built using a method based on Kata for continuous improvement and Kanban for continuous delivery:

  • To achieve our goals and create new ones we have Kata meetings: daily and weekly planning sessions, refinement sessions and weekly demos to show the results. We prefer doing weekly demos to show small and continuous progress, it encourages our team to break down problems into small and more deliverable pieces.
  • On the refinement calls we discuss big pieces of the business logic defined in epics and create smaller stories for them and prepare some technical details. So that later our developers will better understand the story, split it into small subtasks and start to deliver the value step by step.
  • Planning calls are useful to understand and keep right direction and daily calls help to track everyday state of development. You can find more details about Kata and Kanban on our confluence page.

How we design our systems

Our platform is built with many small microservices, which allows the Electron team to work independently on each service. It’s convenient to use such a system for our focus around automation. Also, lots of Pfizer teams are using this approach so we won’t stop to explain what microservices are. You can read more about Pfizer’s microservices here.

It’d be better to explain how we design the communication between services such as between Edison Dashboard and Acquia (Cloudflare, Wormly and others). The answer will be – drumroll – Electron and its event-driven system. Basically, we have the service called electron-event which plays the role of the router in this schema. To simplify you can imagine it like this:

So, Edison Dashboard (or something using our service) sends the event with predefined payload to the common SQS queue. The electron-event service (our router) listens to the queue and routes this event to the special queue which belongs to one of the services. That one in turn picks up the event and runs the appropriate task. Each of the services are responsible to work with a certain endpoint system (e.g., the electron-acquia service works with the Acquia system).

Let’s look at an example with database sync. Edison Dashboard sends a db-sync event and our router redirects it to electron-db service where we start database synchronization process and that’s it.

This has been a quick overview of how Electron uses event-driven design. This approach has many advantages and there are many reasons why we use it. But let’s leave that for the next post.
