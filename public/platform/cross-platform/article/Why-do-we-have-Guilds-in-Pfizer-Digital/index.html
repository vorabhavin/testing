Why do we have Guilds in Pfizer Digital?  

Why do we have Guilds in Pfizer Digital?  

by Platform DevOps

Published: Apr 29, 2021

Guilds: What they are, why we encourage them, and how they promote best practice 


Guilds are one of a handful of specialist communities within Pfizer, and specifically within Pfizer Digital. They exist to support individuals with similar responsibilities and interests and bring a host of benefits.  


What is a Guild?  

A Guild is a self-organizing community of peers with shared interests. It is a group of people across an organization who want to share knowledge, tools, and practices. Guilds are voluntary and exist to serve their members: they are not necessarily a mechanism of governance. Examples of current Guilds in Pfizer Digital include the Test Guild, DevSecOps Guild, and Product Owner Guild.  


Why do we have Guilds?  

Let’s take a step back for a moment. Pfizer’s Digital organization is easily over 2,000 people, with five major teams and around 30 sub-teams. Everything moves at the speed of the business (in other words: fast) and, information-wise, it’s necessarily chaotic at times. People are working hard and quickly on solving problems using innovative digital solutions. Overlapping information or similar problems can crop up – and Guilds are one way of attempting to centralize and pool our extensive collective experiences. This means we can reduce siloing and avoid having to reinvent the wheel.  


Guilds are one type of community support. A large, healthy ecosystem made up of many multi-disciplinary teams (or tribes) has many. For instance, Pfizer Digital also has Office Hours for every digital platform, and Chapters for professionals sharing the same job title (there is both a Developer Chapter and a Delivery Chapter). Guilds are communities of interest where people can opt-in and out - paying attention to them as time permits. Chapters are structures for alignment - focused on items that should be done. But both Guilds and Chapters and are by people, for people, centered around solving shared problems. At Pfizer, they are helping us all to drive for digital maturity. 


How Guilds benefit their members and the wider company 

A Guild’s strength lies in its ability to facilitate specific information-sharing, empowering professionals to work together and solve similar problems. 


A Guild is also an opportunity for individuals and like-minded folks to drive their own professional self-development. People are at the heart of every technology transformation: growing and sharing best practice peer-to-peer has the potential to drive a multitude of positive types of growth. 


Understanding the challenges of implementation 
​​​​​​​

For all their positive impact, Guilds do face challenges.  

  • Culturally, Guilds are not an established, long-lived part of our culture yet, and many may not even be aware of the current existence of Guilds 
  • As a result, Guilds will rely on continued and increased engagement to gain traction – but people are busy! Scheduling in another hour for a metawork meeting can be a lot to ask managers with 40+ hours of meetings already, even if the interest is there 
  • Moving to a model where people self-organize to create their own support structures involves, for many large companies, a culture shift that is necessarily a slow process. However, the support of the DevSecOps and Agile groups within Pfizer definitely promote and support Guilds 
  • Guild start-up and maintenance requires motivation and coordination from its members. Starting with a small, tangible problem and coming together in a group, then iterating, can be a great starting point for this. 


​​​​​​​By design, Guilds to not need to centrally self-register and they grow organically.
