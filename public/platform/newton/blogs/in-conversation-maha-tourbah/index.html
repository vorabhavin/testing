In conversation with Maha Tourbah, Newton Business Analyst

by Platform DevOps

Published: Mar 9, 2021

By incorporating a Business Analyst, Newton has made space to take incisive decisions about improving processes and avoiding costs. Maha explains her role and why this type of insight is critical.

What’s your role at Pfizer?

I’m the lead Business Analyst on the Newton Platform team.

What is Newton?

Newton is Pfizer Digital’s solution to accelerate and simplify development and delivery of mobile applications to patients, customers, healthcare providers and colleagues across mobile and connected health ecosystems. In a nutshell, we make building, delivering and maintaining apps a better experience.

What are you currently working on?

I joined in August 2020 and since then my primary – but far from only – focus has been value (or process) stream mapping.

Mobile development at Pfizer has been adapted multiple times and been through multiple iterations. As a result, there are a plethora of processes, some still in use but also a lot deprecated. Some are documented (some multiple times!) and others are not, or are documented inaccurately. Processes are the foundations of how a team works: they are how we deliver value, and this dictates how much support we generate! Hugh Fidgen (Newton Lead) and the team had a clear goal: understand more, make better decisions, improve multiple stakeholders’ experiences.

My current work involves everything from defining the processes and where (and how) they’re documented, to specifics: how much are they costing us; are they efficient; how can we improve? This is just the top level of questions but gives you an outline.

Give some examples of where you’ve made a positive impact with Newton.

As part of my work, Newton Product Owner Tom DeMaio and I have fully explored and documented the Mobile development journey for the first time. This is no small feat. It’s an end-to-end sketch with a lot of variables and involving a lot of interlinked but separate teams. It starts from requesting an app project, whatever the type; approval; building; environments; project management; support; testing and UAT; deployment; release; maintenance; continuity with Operations. It also covers bugs and problems and user scenarios.

It makes our process tangible and means we can support our users better than ever – and that’s before we even start looking at the data.

Additionally, we now document the roadmap in Jira, and align with components that make sense for usage outside of team’s realm, like the Request for Proposal process (RFP).

We’ve also pushed more for feedback and metrics in the last six months. With our great team, driven by Tom’s insight, we’re now working with Stratus to build our own version of their Dashboard where we can display everything about our platform’s efficacy in one place, including the four key DevOps metrics. This is a clear improvement: without this, users have to send a request via Teams, and consult a bunch of distributed systems. Centralizing and self-serving that information is powerful for users, and for us as a Platform. From this Dashboard, we can easily see: how many visitors we got there, what they asked for, how much time it took them, and so on.

How is your work important to Pfizer? What benefits do you bring to the team?

My work means the difference between educated assumptions and actual calculations. If you want to save money or avoid costs, it’s the right choice to actually crunch the numbers and analytically look at things. I enable the team to put numbers on their assumptions.

My value is being able to approach and appraise the data in relation to the team, so I can start to build up a precise answer to a really important (and incredibly complex) question: where is Newton spending its money? Where can we optimize that?

I’m from a very product-oriented background, so I can look at things and work out from product to epics to components, aligning with the documentation we have in Confluence: how much are we spending per component? In this context, we have created a “Units of Work” model based on our product’s components and the distribution of the team’s effort on each component via what they achieve in sprints.

Being able to track and document things properly – like in Smartsheets, and connected to Github – means you can start to rise out of the “infrastructure swamp” and get actual Platform-specific numbers:

  • How many builds were successful, how many failed and why?
  • How many releases per day are we hitting? Or per week?
  • How many apps are using our services?
  • How does that avoid costs for them, and ultimately for Pfizer?
  • And how does it all come back into the effort we are putting into each component we’re building?
    ​​​​​​​

Once we get more at ease with capturing and processing these numbers, we can start relating them to the business. There are two kinds of metrics: KPIs and SLAs. One is at product level and one at service level. We can start to critically analyze things like: what is the performance of the service? What is the availability, during its supposed availability time? What part of this time was it down? How long did it take to get back up again?

On top of that, the insights relate right back to the team itself, be it the product-centered tier of the team, or the client facing one. Continuous improvement is about professional development and team culture as well as business metrics. In this context, we’ve created a process tracker, across the Newton platform (product and support), to keep our processes population in check and to put numbers on their current cost of execution versus their cost following the optimizations we are working on. Eventually, after documentation and optimization, processes become mature enough to be operationalized or even automated.

What challenges do you face?

The complexity of the ecosystem at Pfizer with regards to all the teams is a challenge. Building up a list of even just the Operations teams involved in supporting and publishing mobile apps was difficult.

What do you like best about being embedded in the Newton team?

It has shown me a whole new product line – mobile apps platform– and allowed me to meet a bunch of highly skilled people. The sheer volume of work to be done is astounding, but the team ploughs on in good spirits, with great collaboration.

What allows you to work successfully?

The support of management, namely Hugh, and great collaboration with Tom and the whole team. I wholeheartedly believe in collective intelligence and working in collaboration, both within the team and across teams. Exchanging ideas is extremely enriching to all involved. I will be creating a BA community, starting small - locally, to share and compare notes on how we can continuously improve by borrowing good ideas and getting support to rid us of debt.
