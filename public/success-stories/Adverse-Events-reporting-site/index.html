Building an Adverse Events reporting site for the COVID era 

Success Story: Building an Adverse Events reporting site for the COVID era

by Platform DevOps

Published: Apr 19, 2021

How Pfizer Digital applied their expertise to support a global Adverse Events reporting (AE) digital solution of unprecedented size and reach.

Pfizer Digital succeeded in providing a global Adverse Events reporting (AE) digital solution of unprecedented size and reach. Here’s how.



Understanding Adverse Events

Let’s start with an explanation. Just what is an Adverse Event (AE)? Put simply, it’s an undesirable experience associated with the use of a medical product in a patient.

In every market and region globally, AEs and other patient safety events must legally be recorded according to specific guidelines.

Taking these measures protects patients, drives accountability, and improves healthcare outcomes.

The challenges

With the advent and rollout of a global COVID-19 vaccine, Pfizer is one of few global leaders in the pharmaceutical and sociological fight against the virus. Today, data and digital solutions are more critical than ever before. You can submit an AE from your phone or computer, connecting from wherever you are.

Pfizer Digital has the skills, expertise, and tools to build complex and tailored solutions.

Building a successful system for this specific situation, however, requires overcoming some non-trivial barriers:

  • Volume: Arguably the most significant technical problem of all. How to build a solution capable of handling 600 concurrent submissions and over 1000 users a minute?

  • No precedent: Building an AE site for a vaccine that could be deployed to every country in the world at record speed had never been done before by Pfizer.

  • Data management: The safety and security of digital data does not take a one-size-fits-all approach. It requires conscientious management as security threats change.

  • Region-specific accuracy: Every country is different in its regulations and mandatory processes (both medical and legal).

Though most people think of the incredible achievement and effort spent rolling out and supporting the vaccine, having a solid AE site is a non-negotiable accompaniment. If this wasn’t implemented correctly or reliably, patients could be negatively impacted, and Pfizer’s reputation significantly damaged.

What we did

Previous experience had taught Pfizer’s Platform DevOps team that the ideal solution was already within our expertise: Static sites built on Edison’s Lite offering, coupled with modular Stratus microservices. This was the approach used (to incredible success) to build and rapidly deploy 30+ COVID-19 vaccine sites at the end of 2020. Over the preceding 18 months, our teams had worked tirelessly to optimize this architecture – and we now know just how strong our approach and teamwork can be.

The solution was built on tried and tested Pfizer platforms:

  • An Edison Lite site – simple, static site using HTML, CSS and Javascript, interoperable with…

  • Stratus microservices – in this case, a well-designed, multi-step Form microservice…

  • Which captures and brokers information to be stored in Argus, an Oracle application focused on pharmacovigilance. In order to integrate Stratus and Argus, an integration software called MuleSoft acts as the link between the two.

Edison Lite static sites plus Stratus microservices offer simplicity and flexibility; they are also incredibly secure and scalable. The questions now were how to tweak the solution to:

  • Cope with expected demand

  • Perform within certain mandated timeframes

Overcoming the challenge of timing

Timing was important in this project. What seemed like a simple idea was incredibly complicated in practice:

  • The form is designed to be completed in 15 minutes: Any longer, and it times out, removing the data. This protects our users who may be completing the form on a public computer.

  • The clock also starts ticking as soon as the form is submitted: The form data must be moved to Argus within 24 hours to stay in compliance with our reporting guidelines. On average it takes about 10 minutes to send the data to Argus and receive an acknowledgement that its safety stored in their database.

  • This meant a detailed system of checks, alerts, and notifications had to be built in to keep on top of a multitude of delays and problems.

What we learned: reusable architecture is Pfizer Digital’s strength

The team focused on architecting their solution to maximize usability and simplicity.

Because the call centers were still experiencing high volume of support requests, a voice bot was approved to verbally submit a user’s AE over the phone. This voice bot was created within the AI team, but it uses the Stratus microservices to move the voice bot-captured AE to Argus. This highlights how repurposable microservices are – designed to be flexible and modular, even when brokering form data over a completely different communication channel.

A successful solution

The site got off to a strong start on December 3rd 2020, and continues to support high usage:

  • Over 50,000 page views to date
  • Over 15,000 AEs submitted
  • Average completion time of just eight minutes
